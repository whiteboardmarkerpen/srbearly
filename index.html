Stolen Rice Bowl

Things Go Well

Chapter 1: 1: Kai (Hua antagonizes Mei)

Kai tends the fire while his mother, Hua, and wife, Mei, prepare dinner. Bored and detached, he reflects on their modest meals and recalls the more flavorful dishes from the night market, which he cannot afford. When Hua announces dinner, Kai dutifully helps set the table. The trio eats in silence, observing customs that honor Hua. As rain unexpectedly falls, interrupting Kai’s plans to work the fields, Hua praises his hard work, trying to encourage Mei, who remains reserved. Hua then theatrically offers Kai the remainder of her rice, turning the moment into one of playful affection. He accepts, appreciating her gesture without question.

* * *

Hua pressures Mei to finish her bitter Dong Quai tea, stressing its importance for fertility. Mei, feeling overwhelmed by the tension, hesitates, staring at the cup, remembering past moments of Hua's cruelty, especially after her miscarriage. Hua had mocked the loss publicly, while Mei suffered silently.

Kai, observing Mei’s discomfort, recalls how she struggles with the tea’s nauseating taste, and how Hua won’t let her eat until it’s finished. He briefly reflects on their youth and the unnecessary urgency Hua places on conceiving. Despite Mei's attempt to comply, Hua’s disappointment looms, escalating the emotional strain in the room.

* * *

In the past, Mei’s father raised her with ambitions of greatness, teaching her skills in reading, writing, and business, and sharing stories of his global trading expeditions, which fueled her curiosity about the world. When her parents left for Nanjing at the outbreak of the Second Sino-Japanese War, Mei was left with her uncle, waiting anxiously for their return. After news of the Nanjing Massacre, hope faded, and her uncle took advantage of the situation, seizing her father’s assets and arranging her marriage to Kai, a young farmer, for his own business interests. Despite the modest wedding, tensions arose in their marriage, especially as Kai’s mother, Hua, fixated on Mei’s duty to bear children. Kai defended Mei against his mother’s harshness, but his words only intensified Hua’s anger.

Back in the dinner, Mei, caught in the conflict, complies with Hua’s demands, drinking the bitter herbal teas meant to aid conception. Hua’s fury melts when Mei submitted, but the pressure to continue the family line weighed heavily on the couple’s relationship, setting a tone of emotional strain.

Chapter 2: 2: Mei (Convinces Kai to leave)

Kai awakens to an unsettling silence, noticing the absence of his mother Hua’s usual morning routine. He finds Mei, in the kitchen, lost in thought as she stirs porridge. The tension from Hua's outburst the previous night lingers, casting a shadow over the household. As they eat breakfast together, Kai reflects on the rain-soaked fields, realizing they can't till the land, which frustrates him. He yearns for action to break the monotony of their impoverished life. In a bid to distract himself, Kai proposes to Mei that they go "treasure hunting," seeking any form of escape from their stagnant situation.

* * *

Mei, once disgusted by the idea of going to the river, had never participated in tasks like disposing of trash, which were left to servants in her previous life. However, after her arranged marriage to Kai, her perspective changed. Kai introduced the river not as a place of disposal but as a source of treasure. On their first visit, Mei found a beautiful, intact ceramic jar, prompting an emotional reflection on her father’s disappearance during the Second Sino-Japanese War. In that vulnerable moment, Kai offered comfort, assuring her that he would take care of her. This marked the beginning of their treasure-hunting adventures. Three years later, despite Hua’s lingering anger from the previous night, Mei agreed to join Kai again, hoping to discover something meaningful.

* * *

Kai and Mei walk to the river under a shared umbrella, the tension from the previous night still lingering. As they search through debris for anything of value, Kai tries to lighten the mood with playful antics, while Mei remains distant. Suddenly, Mei experiences a brief stomach pain, which Kai interprets as a possible pregnancy, but she dismisses the idea, expressing her disillusionment with the world. However, Mei finds a small jade stone, sparking excitement and nostalgia for her mother’s jade jewelry, which had been kept by her uncle after her parents' deaths. The discovery of the jade and some fish lightens the mood, and they decide to return home, feeling that the jade might be a sign of good luck.

* * *

Mei brings up the idea of moving to Kuala Lumpur for a better life, suggesting that Kai could work in the tin mines, which are more lucrative than farming. Kai, initially confused, recalls how Mei once mentioned this idea, but Hua had shut it down. He feels torn between his traditional role as a farmer and village chief, passed down through generations, and Mei’s desire for a different future. Mei expresses her longing for a better life for their children, beyond the hard labor and limited rewards of farming. Though Kai promises to work even harder and pursue new opportunities, Mei feels her hope slipping, realizing that Kai’s efforts alone might not be enough to change their circumstances. She resigns herself to his plan, letting the conversation drift away.

Chapter 3: 3: Kai (Mun warns Kai about British)

Kai works the fields with his hoe, finding satisfaction in the rhythmic task of tilling the soil. His mind drifts to thoughts of future harvests and the transformation of the landscape from barren to thriving with rice sprouts, providing him a sense of progress. As he's lost in thought, his cousin Mun surprises him, teasing him for being distracted. Mun encourages Kai to relax and join the family for his 20th birthday celebration, offering to have the buffalo till the field for him the next day. Although tempted by the offer, Kai hesitates, feeling the weight of responsibility and past memories of his mother's critical voice. He recalls moments from his childhood when he felt inadequate, burdened by the constant pressure to prove himself.

* * *

Kai declines Mun's offer to use his father's buffalo for tilling, preferring to work the field himself. Mun, instead of leaving, picks up a hoe to help Kai, calling it his birthday present. The cousins work in silence, reminiscent of a bonding moment after Kai’s father died when they climbed a tree together. Mun brings up Mei’s jade gemstone find, teasing Kai about working harder because of it and about losing face when Mei outperformed him by catching fish. Kai reveals that he wants Mei to keep the jade for a ring, but Mun suggests selling it for something useful, like a buffalo. Despite the teasing, Kai insists that Mei should keep what she found, feeling it’s important to honor his commitment to her.

* * *

Mun encourages Kai to work in the tin mine, promising higher and more consistent pay than farming. Mun argues that farming is no longer profitable, with cheaper imports and the threat of the British government eventually taking their land. He paints tin mining as exciting and lucrative, contrasting it with the challenges and solitude of farming. However, Kai is deeply attached to their family’s legacy of farming, recalling the stories his mother told about their ancestors who settled and cultivated the land. Despite the financial appeal of mining, Kai feels a sense of duty to continue the farming tradition. He rejects Mun’s offer, determined to make farming work and preserve their heritage.

Chapter 4: 4: Hua (Pines after dead husband and baby)

Hua reflects on her late husband Bon and how their son, Kai, has now turned twenty. She often "talks" to Bon, updating him about the village and questioning why he left. While Bon was carefree in life, Hua held onto the traditions and beliefs passed down through generations, which she feels have kept her life steady and meaningful. She sometimes feels angry at Bon for not respecting these customs, even though she knows it’s futile to argue with the dead. As she leads a tai chi group, feeling powerful and respected by the community, she acknowledges that her authority is now her own, no longer tied to being the chief’s wife. She is proud of her status as a respected sifu (teacher), though she wishes her daughter-in-law shared the same respect for her.

* * *

Hua’s first pregnancy was initially filled with excitement and hope for the future, but it ended in tragedy when her daughter was stillborn. The shock and grief overwhelmed her, as she had never considered such an outcome. Hua’s family was initially supportive, but soon her mother brought in a traditional expert to investigate what went wrong. The man blamed Hua’s actions during pregnancy, from using a knife to eating certain "forbidden" fruits like watermelon and pineapple, which supposedly disrupted the body’s balance and led to her child’s death. Each new superstition added to Hua's guilt and self-doubt, leaving her to question everything she had done. Her mother, who believed Hua had lived too carelessly before, saw this transformation as necessary, feeling that Hua’s arrogance had been broken by the tragedy. The experience left Hua feeling burdened with guilt and a deepened belief in the importance of following traditional customs to avoid further loss.

* * *

After Hua's daughter's death, village gossip turned her tragedy into a spectacle, with many believing the man's traditional customs were a return to ancestral practices. Hua blamed herself for not following them, spiraling into guilt, believing her actions had invited evil spirits. One night, she broke down, confessing to her husband Bon that she felt unworthy of being a mother. He comforted her as she wept, until she apologized and fell asleep. During her pregnancy with Kai, Hua strictly followed every custom, feeling prepared and empowered. Kai's healthy birth affirmed her belief in these traditions, and over the next twenty years, she became a respected figure in the village. Leading a tai chi group, she reflected on her success and was determined that her daughter-in-law, Mei, would eventually follow these customs too.

Chapter 5: 5: Mei (Discovers Pregnancy)

Hua instructs Mei to prepare wild orchids for the family dining table, their vibrant beauty mirroring Hua’s commanding presence. Mei, who prefers the simple elegance of frangipanis, reflects on how Hua once forbade her from bringing those flowers indoors due to superstitions. As Mei silently arranges the orchids, she thinks about how freely she communicates with Kai, while in Hua’s presence, she feels silenced. The tension continues in the kitchen, where Mei struggles to meet Hua’s high standards for cooking. Despite years of effort, Mei finds herself continually criticized, especially for not cooking to perfection, a sharp contrast to her upbringing where she was encouraged to focus on education and creativity rather than domestic duties. The dissonance between her past life and current reality weighs on her as she navigates Hua’s demanding household.

* * *

As Mei prepares flowers at Hua's request, a small cat appears, and Mei tries to discreetly shoo it away, knowing Hua disapproves of cats, especially black ones, due to superstition. When Hua notices, she accuses Mei of bringing bad luck by allowing the cat near, even blaming the cat for Mei's failure to conceive. Hua tells a story about why there is no Year of the Cat in the Chinese zodiac, reinforcing her belief in bad omens. Mei tries to defend herself but ultimately backs down to avoid further conflict. As Mei excuses herself for a break, Hua intrusively asks about Mei’s menstrual cycle, making Mei uncomfortable and recalling how menstruation had always been a hushed, awkward subject. Mei longs for the ease and friendship she had in the city, now feeling isolated on the farm and stifled by Hua’s constant scrutiny.

* * *

Mei asks Hua if she can leave, feeling vulnerable, but Hua keeps her, excitedly suggesting that Mei might be pregnant. Hua bases this on noticing Mei’s recent nausea and frequent nighttime trips to the bathroom. Despite Mei's discomfort, Hua places a hand on her abdomen, convinced that her prescribed herbal teas and traditional methods have led to a potential pregnancy. While Hua is overjoyed, Mei contemplates the possibility of being pregnant herself, viewing it as a potential opportunity to convince Kai to leave farm life and return to the city. However, Hua insists that it’s too early to tell Kai or anyone else, warning that speaking about it too soon could invite bad luck. Hua's dominance in their lives continues to weigh on Mei, who dreams of a different future.

Chapter 6: 6: Kai (Mun beats Kai at soccer)

In a spirited game of soccer in the muddy fields, Kai and his cousin Mun lead opposing teams in a close match. Kai finds freedom and exhilaration in the game, contrasting with his usual farming duties. As the sun sets and the teams are tied, tension builds between Kai and Mun, reminiscent of childhood rivalries. Kai is determined to win, fueled by unspoken feelings of resentment toward Mun, who left the village for the city. In a critical moment, Kai impulsively decides not to pass the ball, hoping to score himself, but Mun intercepts and scores the winning goal. Mun is celebrated by his teammates, while Kai is left feeling defeated, not only by the loss but by his failure to trust his teammates and the deeper emotions stirred by the rivalry with his cousin.

* * *

After the soccer game, as Kai and Mun washed the mud off by the well, Seng questioned why Kai hadn’t passed the ball, teasingly suggesting that Kai wanted the glory for himself on his birthday. Kai, though smiling, was inwardly troubled by the loss and his choices during the game. After the others left, Kai and Mun lingered, and Mun tried to lighten the mood, but Kai shifted the conversation to his mother’s constant pressure on Mei to have a child, expressing frustration over the hurt caused by these expectations, especially after Mei’s miscarriage. Mun, trying to help, suggested an herbal tea remedy, but Kai, already overwhelmed by Hua’s insistence on herbal solutions, lashed out in frustration. Mun apologized, emphasizing his intention to support Kai as family. Kai, realizing he had unfairly vented on his cousin, softened and thanked him, appreciating his cousin’s sincere offer of help.

* * *

Kai arrives home to a lively family gathering filled with the comforting smells and sounds of his childhood. He watches his younger cousins playing a game and his older relatives in loud but familiar conversation. Passing familiar symbols from his past, like a wau kite on the flagpole he built with Mun, Kai feels a mix of nostalgia and responsibility.

As Kai interacts with family members, including Mun’s mother, who teases him about turning twenty, and his cousin, who curiously asks about adulthood, he plays the part of the good host. Despite feeling out of place, Kai knows his mother, Hua, wanted this celebration for him, so he hides his discomfort with forced smiles and pleasantries.

When Kai finally gets to spend time with Mei, their playful exchange brings him comfort. Amid their teasing, she confides in him with a serious revelation: she’s pregnant, leaving Kai stunned in the midst of the chaos of the celebration.

Chapter 7: 7: Hua (Slapping Mei)

Hua prepares for her son Kai’s twentieth birthday by smoothing her cheongsam and placing a protective talisman on the backyard's wooden frame, a prayer for his future. As she lights incense and offers silent petitions to her ancestors, she anxiously awaits Kai’s arrival. As guests gather, including her comforting in-laws, Beng and Song, Hua grows more concerned about Kai's absence. The conversation shifts to family matters, with light-hearted jokes about Kai's dedication to his work and his close bond with his friend Mun. Discussions about rising prices due to the ongoing war add a layer of tension to the festive mood. Despite her outward composure, Hua’s worry about her son lingers, even as she exchanges pleasantries about the evening’s preparations.

* * *

Hua, Song, and Beng discuss the rising costs of living and the influence of British colonial powers, particularly their push for Chinese families to return to working in tin mines. This sparks a conversation about the broader implications for their community, and Hua expresses fierce loyalty to their land, determined that her son Kai will not leave to work elsewhere. Song reveals rumors that Kai’s wife, Mei, wants to move to the city, which unsettles Hua. Despite outward confidence that Kai is committed to their village, Hua's growing concern over Mei's influence leads her to resolve to confront her daughter-in-law.

* * *

Hua confronts Mei about rumors of her and Kai leaving the village for the city. Mei confirms they’ve considered it, citing better opportunities and concerns for their future child. The conversation grows tense as Hua reacts with disbelief, defending the traditions and sacrifices tied to village life. Mei, however, stands firm, asserting her right to choose what’s best for their family. The conflict highlights Hua’s struggle between holding onto the past and facing the modern ambitions Mei represents. As their argument escalates, Hua pulls back to avoid making a scene in front of the guests, but she remains deeply upset, feeling disrespected and betrayed. Returning to her seat, Hua vents to Beng and Song, who sympathize with her, attributing Mei's defiance to her city upbringing and the younger generation’s perceived arrogance.

* * *

Hua observes Kai and Mei sharing a private moment, triggering her fear that they plan to leave her behind for the city. Her worry intensifies when she overhears Mei announcing her pregnancy, which enrages Hua as it goes against tradition to speak of such things so early. Struggling to contain her emotions, Hua confronts Mei in front of the gathered guests. Her anger boils over, leading her to slap Mei across the face. Mei, stunned and hurt, runs out of the house. The festive atmosphere is shattered, and the room falls into a tense silence. Kai, initially frozen in shock, is urged by Mun to follow Mei. He rushes after her, leaving Hua grappling with the aftermath of her outburst, bolstered momentarily by the subtle approval of Beng and Song, yet haunted by the consequences of her actions.

Chapter 8: 8: Kai (Chasing Mei)

Kai races through his sleeping village and into the forest to catch up with his wife, Mei, who has run off after being slapped by his mother, Hua. He finds Mei by a lake, distressed and hurt by the public humiliation. Hua had slapped her for announcing her pregnancy to Kai, believing it was bad luck to reveal such news too soon. As Kai tries to comfort Mei, he unintentionally sides with his mother's superstitions, which angers Mei further. She expresses frustration over Hua's actions and feels unsupported by Kai. Realizing the depth of her emotions, Kai chooses to remain silent, sensing it's not the right moment to argue.

* * *

Mei expresses her frustration over Hua’s superstition and control, emphasizing the joy of her pregnancy and her disbelief at being told to keep it a secret. Kai reflects on his own past, recalling how his parents kept him in the dark about his father’s illness, leading to a sense of alienation. He admits that Hua was wrong to slap Mei but tries to defend her beliefs, claiming she wants respect and that he’ll talk to her. Mei, however, remains unconvinced, demanding an apology from Hua, pointing out that she never apologizes to anyone. Kai reflects on his mother's rigid pride and the generational divide, unsure if he, too, would struggle with apologizing as a parent. Mei insists they must break the cycle and be different, challenging Kai’s views on parenthood.

* * *

Mei confronts Kai, demanding he choose between her and his mother, frustrated by his continued deference to Hua despite the slap. She emphasizes her need to be prioritized, especially now that she’s pregnant. Kai, torn between his loyalty to his mother and his commitment to Mei, struggles with the idea of cutting Hua off. Mei proposes moving to the city for a fresh start, free from the superstitions and control of village life. While Kai expresses discomfort with the city’s busyness and lack of nature, Mei counters with its conveniences, excitement, and the opportunities it offers for a better life. She longs for a new beginning and urges Kai to embrace the change.

* * *

Kai wrestles with the idea of moving to the city and working in the tin mines, feeling torn between the comfort of his village life and Mei’s vision of a better future. Mei insists that as long as they stay, Hua will continue mistreating her, and she pushes Kai to prioritize her and their unborn child. She compares him to a bird tethered by his mother's influence, urging him to break free and explore a world beyond the village.

Kai is hesitant, defending his mother’s role in shaping his hard work and expressing doubt about moving to the city. Mei points out that the British tin mines offer higher pay and better opportunities than the Chinese tin mines, arguing that their future children would benefit from education and a more modern lifestyle. She criticizes his cousin Mun for flaunting luxuries from the tin mines, which fuels Kai’s competitive spirit. Despite his lingering doubts, Kai starts considering the possibility of a better life in the city, though he remains cautious about Mei’s bold assertions.

* * *

Mei tells Kai the British are phasing out Chinese farming for tin mining, with the village Chief offering irrigation in exchange for workers. Though intrigued, Kai hesitates due to his recent efforts on the fields. Mei urges him to reconsider, stressing they can't return to Hua after the slap. Reluctantly, Kai agrees to meet the Chief, swayed by Mei's belief in him, contrasting with Hua's criticism. Acknowledging Mei's own motives, Kai sides with her, valuing her support. When Mei insists she won't return home, Kai apologizes for his mother, and Mei sees this as a chance for him to truly understand her.

* * *

Kai suggests spending the night in a farmer's barn to escape the tension at home, and Mei eagerly agrees, seeing it as a fresh start. As they settle in, Kai makes a bed, while Mei finds comfort in a stray black cat, reminding him of his mother's superstitions. Watching Mei's tenderness with the cat, Kai contrasts her unconditional love with his mother's conditional care. As he kisses Mei, he realizes the depth of his love and their impending parenthood. Despite his feelings for Mei, thoughts of his mother’s slap and their uncertain future in the tin mines weigh on him.

Chapter 9: 9: Mei (Breakfast with Mun)

Mei wakes to the gentle light of dawn filtering through the bamboo beams, surprised to see Kai up early. He enters with a straw hat and a bag of clothes, sparking Mei's curiosity. They discuss his early visit to their family home, where he avoided his mother after her violent slap the day before. Tension rises as Mei recalls the slap and questions his return, but Kai reassures her by revealing their clothes and a treasured jade piece. They plan to use the jade to fund their escape, with Kai suggesting they speak to Chief Tienpao about a tin mine opportunity. Mei, torn between the practicalities of their journey and her longing for city life, prepares for the day ahead, silently reflecting on the differences between her and Kai.

* * *

Mun enters the barn, worried about Kai and Mei. He notices the mark on Mei's cheek from Hua’s slap and expresses concern. Offering steamed buns and jasmine tea, Mun lightens the mood with his congratulations on Mei’s pregnancy, prompting her to reflect on her new role as a mother. As they eat together, Mun suggests they consider moving to Ipoh, where there are opportunities in the tin mines. However, Kai’s competitive nature surfaces, and tension lingers from their previous interactions. Mei, sensing Kai’s discomfort, shifts the conversation by asking Mun about the Chinese tin mines, seeking to understand the situation better.

* * *

Mun questions Mei’s mention of "Chinese" tin mines and realizes they might be considering British-controlled mines. He expresses distrust of British promises, reminding them of unfulfilled land ownership agreements and warning that working for the British could strip them of their cultural identity. Mei, however, advocates for the higher pay and opportunities associated with the British mines, drawing on her experiences in Penang. Mun tries to convince them that staying with the Chinese mines would preserve their heritage and be less disruptive for Kai, but Mei grows increasingly frustrated with Mun's condescending tone. Kai, breaking his silence, reminds Mun of his past abandonment, casting a tense silence over the conversation.

* * *

Kai acknowledges Mun’s point that he might fit in better at the Chinese tin mines but insists on considering the village’s needs, noting the British promises to build canals to benefit the fields. Mun dismisses these promises, arguing for leaving farming behind in favor of modernization, while Kai stresses the importance of responsibility and community. The conversation escalates as Kai confronts Mun about his sudden departure years ago, which left the family struggling, and the emotional toll it took, especially on Mun’s mother. Mun defends his actions, revealing his feelings of inadequacy compared to Kai and expressing his desire to break free from expectations. Despite Mun's attempt to rekindle their bond, Kai’s bitterness prevails, and he rejects Mun’s offer to work together, telling him to leave. Mun departs, hurt and angry, as the room is left thick with unresolved tension. Mei, witnessing the clash, feels a mixture of satisfaction and hope, as Kai seems more inclined toward the British proposal.

Despite her concerns, Hua decides to trust Song and rejoin the celebration to prevent rumors. Overwhelmed by anxiety, Hua distracts herself with cleaning, singing lullabies as a means of grounding herself. However, as she sits alone, her worry intensifies. Junrong then proposes an alternative: Mei could work for him while saving money before joining Kai in Kuala Lumpur. Kai opposes the idea, concerned for Mei’s safety and her pregnancy, but Junrong assures him that Mei would stay with his family. Mei, intrigued by the possibility of working with Junrong's family, considers the offer, despite Kai’s discomfort. They agree to pawn the jade and secure the village chief's endorsement before making a final decision.

Chapter 12: 12: Mei (Exchanging Jade piece at Pawnshop)

Mei and Kai arrive at a pawnshop that radiates a haunting atmosphere, filled with the stories of past patrons. As they prepare to present a jade piece, Mei instructs Kai to claim it was her mother's, avoiding the truth that they found it in the river.
